In this practical, I found the first two tasks easier than the final spatial SIR task. The simple SIR model and the vaccination model were easier because they mainly used variables, lists, loops, random choices, and plotting, so the logic was quite direct once I understood how susceptible, infected, recovered, and vaccinated people should change over time. 

The last task was more difficult because it used a two-dimensional grid, and I had to think carefully about positions, neighbours, edges of the grid, infection, recovery, and how to save different time points for plotting. When writing the final code, I learned many new functions and pieces of syntax, such as `np.zeros()` for making a grid, `np.where()` for finding infected cells, `.copy()` for saving a population state without it changing later, and `plt.subplot()` with `plt.imshow()` for showing several heat maps in one figure.

I also learned why checking boundaries is important in a spatial model, because cells at the edge of the grid do not have all eight neighbours. The most important thing I learned was that a biological model can be built step by step from simple rules, and even a small change, such as vaccination or spatial contact, can make the behaviour of the model much more interesting and realistic.
